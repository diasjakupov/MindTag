1. Principles of Scarcity

Scarcity refers to the basic economic problem, the gap between limited resources and theoretically limitless wants. This situation requires people to make decisions about how to allocate resources efficiently, in order to satisfy basic needs and as many additional wants as possible.

Fig 1.1

Any resource that has a non-zero cost to consume is scarce to some degree, but what matters in practice is relative scarcity.

  • Land: Natural resources used for production.
  • Labor: Human effort directed toward producing goods and services.
  • Capital: Tools, equipment, and factories used in production.

2. Opportunity Cost

Because resources are scarce, every choice has an opportunity cost. The opportunity cost of an activity is the value of the next-best alternative that must be forgone.

For example, if you spend time studying for economics, you cannot spend that same time studying for history or sleeping. The value of the history study or sleep you missed is the opportunity cost.
